Security foundations

CIA Triad, vulnerability, threat, and risk.

Strong security decisions start with clear language: what must be protected, what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how much damage it could cause.

C

Confidentiality

Only authorized people and systems can access sensitive data.

I

Integrity

Information and systems stay accurate, complete, and trustworthy.

A

Availability

Critical services remain accessible when people need them.

Vulnerability

A weakness that can be exploited, such as missing patches, weak passwords, exposed services, or insecure code.

Threat

A person, event, or condition that can exploit a vulnerability, including attackers, malware, insiders, errors, or outages.

Risk

The potential business impact when a threat can exploit a vulnerability against an important asset.

1. Identify Assets, data, systems, dependencies, and owners.
2. Assess Vulnerabilities, threats, likelihood, impact, and current controls.
3. Prioritize Rank risk by severity, exposure, exploitability, and business value.
4. Treat Mitigate, transfer, avoid, or accept risk with accountable decisions.
5. Monitor Review changes, incidents, control health, and recurring exceptions.

Network quick reference

Traffic, ports, controls, and packet thinking.

Networking is the common ground for SOC, incident response, cloud security, penetration testing, and threat hunting.

Core Concepts

  • OSI and TCP/IP models explain where protocols and controls operate.
  • IP addressing, subnetting, routing, and NAT explain reachability.
  • DNS, HTTP, TLS, SMTP, SMB, SSH, and RDP appear often in investigations.
  • Latency, packet loss, and retransmissions can separate network faults from attacks.

Common Ports

20/21 FTP 22 SSH 25 SMTP 53 DNS 80 HTTP 443 HTTPS 445 SMB 514 Syslog 3389 RDP
Full IANA port and protocol registry

Security Controls

  • Firewalls and ACLs restrict traffic by source, destination, port, and protocol.
  • IDS detects suspicious activity; IPS can block or modify traffic inline.
  • VPNs protect remote traffic through encrypted tunnels.
  • Segmentation limits lateral movement and reduces blast radius.

Packet Analysis Flow

  1. Know the expected baseline.
  2. Capture traffic at the right point.
  3. Filter by host, port, protocol, or time.
  4. Inspect sessions, payload clues, and errors.
  5. Document findings and next action.

Research workflow

Turn findings into evidence, context, and action.

Good cybersecurity research separates facts from assumptions, connects evidence to risk, and ends with a clear recommendation.

Research Steps

  1. Question: Define what you are investigating.
  2. Scope: Set boundaries, systems, dates, and exclusions.
  3. Collect: Gather logs, packets, advisories, artifacts, and source material.
  4. Validate: Cross-check claims and separate confirmed facts from assumptions.
  5. Map: Connect findings to CIA, risk, ATT&CK, Kill Chain, Diamond, CVE, or CVSS.
  6. Report: Explain impact, evidence, severity, and recommended remediation.
